# Decision memo

**Conclusion:** The proposed claim is not supported. The evidence is consistent with a possible quality benefit, but it does not establish that AI coding assistants cause a 30% reduction in software defects.

The vendor case series supplies the 30% figure, but its six customers were selected by undisclosed criteria, had no control group, and increased deployments by 55%. Its before-and-after ticket count therefore cannot isolate the assistant’s effect. The internal comparison found 18% fewer defects per release, but assistant users were more senior and increased automated test coverage by 20%; either could explain some or all of the difference. The randomized study supports faster completion, not fewer defects: accuracy did not differ significantly on isolated tasks. The survey measures belief rather than defects. The outage postmortem identifies a real failure mode but cannot estimate the average effect.

**Decision:** Do not use “AI coding assistants reduce software defects by 30%.” If public wording is needed now, use: “Some observational evidence suggests AI coding assistants may be associated with fewer reported defects, but a causal reduction has not been established.”

**Next evidence:** Run a preregistered randomized or stepped-wedge study in real development teams. Compare escaped defects per release and per deployment, including severity, against a concurrent control. Measure seniority, test coverage, workload, release frequency, assistant use, and AI-attributed incidents, and report uncertainty.
