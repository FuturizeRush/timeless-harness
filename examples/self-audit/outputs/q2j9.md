# Decision memo

**Strongest defensible conclusion:** The supplied evidence does not establish that AI coding assistants reduce software defects, much less by 30%. The 30% figure comes from an uncontrolled vendor case series with undisclosed selection criteria and a simultaneous 55% increase in deployments. The randomized study found faster completion but did not detect an accuracy difference. The internal 18% association is confounded by greater user seniority and 20% higher automated test coverage. The survey measures belief, not defects; the outage postmortem shows a possible failure mode, not the net effect.

**Decision:** Do not publicly claim, “AI coding assistants reduce software defects by 30%.” It presents an unproven association as a general causal effect. A defensible alternative is: “Some observational reports associate AI-assistant use with fewer reported defects, but controlled evidence has not established a reduction.”

**Next evidence:** Run a preregistered, adequately powered, multi-team randomized rollout. Define defects and severity in advance; measure validated production defects relative to deployment or code-change volume; record assistant use, developer seniority, and test coverage; and track AI-attributable incidents. Report absolute rates, uncertainty, and results by team. This would test causality, the size of any effect, and whether benefits outweigh new failure modes.
