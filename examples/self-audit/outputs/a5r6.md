# Decision memo

**Conclusion:** The evidence does not support a causal, generalizable claim that AI coding assistants reduce software defects by 30%. The vendor’s 30% figure comes from a selected, uncontrolled case series during a 55% deployment increase, so changes in workload, reporting, or customer selection could explain the result. The randomized study demonstrates faster task completion, not fewer defects. Internally, assistant use is associated with 18% fewer defects per release, but seniority and a 20% increase in test coverage are plausible alternative explanations. Developer perceptions are not defect measurements, and the outage postmortem shows that assistants can also introduce material risk.

**Decision:** Do not publish the proposed claim. A defensible alternative is: “Some studies and internal observations suggest AI coding assistants may improve developer productivity and code quality, but their effect on software defects is not yet established.”

**Next evidence:** Run a preregistered, adequately powered field study across representative teams. Randomize assistant access where feasible; otherwise use matched teams with comparable seniority, systems, release cadence, and baseline defect rates. Define defects in advance, measure them per deployment and per release, track test coverage and assistant usage, include production incidents, and follow teams long enough to detect delayed defects. Independent analysis and replication should precede any numeric public claim.
